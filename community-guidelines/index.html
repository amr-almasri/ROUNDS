Community Guidelines

A professional room for discussing evidence.

Rounds discussions should help clinicians understand the literature—not expose patients, attack colleagues, promote undisclosed interests, or substitute opinion for evidence.

1. Core principles

Respect

Address the argument

Challenge methods, interpretation, and evidence without attacking the person.

Privacy

No identifiable patients

Do not post details that could identify a patient, even when names are omitted.

Evidence

Separate fact from opinion

Cite sources where practical and state uncertainty, limitations, and conflicts.

Safety

Use the controls

Report concerning content and block members when you need immediate separation.

Participation in Rounds discussion requires agreement to these standards. The Terms of Use also apply.

2. Protect patient privacy

Rounds is not a patient record or clinical messaging system.

Never post information that identifies—or could reasonably be combined to identify—a patient.

Prohibited information includes:

  • names, initials, addresses, email addresses, phone numbers, record numbers, or account numbers;
  • exact dates of birth, admission, discharge, surgery, or death;
  • recognizable photographs, imaging labels, tattoos, voices, or distinctive physical features;
  • rare diagnoses, precise locations, occupations, family relationships, or timelines that make a person identifiable;
  • screenshots or copied text from an electronic health record; and
  • any information restricted by your institution, regulator, professional duty, or applicable privacy law.

When discussing a clinical concept, use a genuinely fictional or sufficiently generalized example. If the educational point depends on identifiable details, do not post it on Rounds.

3. Maintain professional conduct

Do not post or engage in:

  • harassment, intimidation, threats, stalking, or encouragement of self-harm;
  • hate speech or degrading treatment based on identity or protected characteristics;
  • sexual exploitation, graphic sexual content, or unwanted sexual attention;
  • impersonation, credential misrepresentation, fabricated affiliation, or deceptive identity;
  • doxxing or disclosure of another member’s private information;
  • retaliation against someone for reporting or blocking; or
  • coordinated disruption, dogpiling, or repeated off-topic provocation.

Criticism can be direct. It must remain relevant, evidence-focused, and proportionate.

4. Discuss evidence honestly

  • Represent study design, findings, uncertainty, and limitations accurately.
  • Do not describe a Rounds Evidence Score as proof that a study is correct or clinically applicable.
  • Do not fabricate quotations, citations, data, outcomes, credentials, or clinical experience.
  • Clearly distinguish personal experience from research evidence.
  • Disclose relevant financial, consulting, research, institutional, product, or authorship interests.
  • Correct material errors when you become aware of them.

Educational discussion is not individualized medical advice. Do not direct another member to diagnose or treat a specific identifiable patient through Rounds.

5. Keep credentials and systems safe

Never post passwords, verification codes, API keys, private keys, bearer tokens, institutional credentials, or confidential security information. Do not use comments or reports for spam, advertising floods, phishing, malware, automated posting, or manipulation of moderation systems.

Limited, relevant disclosure of a product or service may be acceptable when it directly contributes to the literature discussion and the relationship is clearly disclosed. Undisclosed promotion is not.

6. Respect copyright and institutional access

  • Link to a paper instead of posting its full text.
  • Quote only what is reasonably necessary and legally permitted.
  • Do not upload or distribute paywalled PDFs through comments.
  • Do not share proxy links, access credentials, or methods to evade publisher controls.
  • Respect author, publisher, institution, image, and trademark rights.

7. Reporting and blocking

Report

Report a comment when it may violate these Guidelines. Choose the closest reason and do not submit duplicate or retaliatory reports. Reports go to administrators for review.

Block

Blocking is immediate and two-way within discussion: you stop seeing each other’s comments, and neither account can reply within the other’s conversation. Manage blocked members from Profile → Blocked members.

Immediate danger

Rounds is not monitored as an emergency channel. If someone may be in immediate danger, contact local emergency services or the appropriate institutional authority.

8. How moderation works

Rounds uses narrow automated safeguards and human review:

  • high-confidence patient identifiers, exposed credentials, private keys, and obvious spam may be rejected before publication;
  • ambiguous high-risk content may be hidden and placed in the moderation queue;
  • administrators can review the full reported comment and may restore, hide, or remove it; and
  • automated filtering is a safeguard, not a final judgment about a person or study.

Depending on severity, context, history, and risk, Rounds may issue a warning, limit participation, hold or remove content, suspend access, or terminate an account. Severe violations may be acted on immediately.

Review target

We aim to review ordinary reports within two business days and prioritize credible threats, exposed credentials, and patient-identifying information. This is a target, not a guarantee of continuous monitoring.

Questions, appeals, or urgent safety concerns

Contact Rounds with the relevant comment or report identifier, a concise explanation, and “Community” in the subject line. Do not resend prohibited content or patient-identifying information.
